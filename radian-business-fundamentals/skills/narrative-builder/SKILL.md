# narrative-builder

## When to use

- Pre-fundraise (narrative is the deck's spine)
- Post-positioning audit (positioning needs a narrative skin)
- Every pitch deck rewrites the "why now" slide from scratch
- Recruiting / sales / marketing each describe the company differently

## Core principle

**Narrative-led pitch decks consistently outperform feature-led decks in close and raise rates** (Andy Raskin). Narrative is the connective tissue between positioning, product, and marketing. Without it, each function speaks its own language.

## Andy Raskin's 5-act structure (the proven frame)

1. **Name the change in the world** — undeniable, big, recent
2. **Show the stakes** — who wins, who loses, given that change
3. **Tease the promised land** — a future state the audience wants
4. **Introduce the obstacles** — what's keeping people from the promised land
5. **Position your offering as the path** — but always as a method/movement, not just a product

## Workflow

1. Diagnose current narrative state:
   - Pull pitch deck, sales deck, website hero, recruiting page
   - Where are the "why now" slides? Are they consistent?
   - Run them past 3 buyers / candidates / investors — do they remember the same story?

2. Build the macro narrative (5-act):
   - Workshop with CEO + key stakeholders
   - Validate the change is real (data backs it)
   - Validate the stakes are visceral
   - Validate the promised land matters
   - Articulate the obstacles
   - Frame the offering as the path

3. Produce derivatives:
   - Pitch deck spine
   - Sales deck rewrites
   - Website hero copy
   - Founder LinkedIn manifesto
   - Recruiting page narrative
   - Investor update intro

4. Distribute internally — every function should articulate the same narrative

5. Track resonance — buyer/candidate/investor recall of key narrative beats

## Output format

```markdown
*Narrative — [Company]*

**Act 1 — The change:**
[1-2 sentence statement of the undeniable shift]
*Evidence:* [3 data points or quotes proving it]

**Act 2 — The stakes:**
[Who wins, who loses, why this matters now]

**Act 3 — The promised land:**
[The future state the buyer / market wants — be vivid]

**Act 4 — The obstacles:**
[What's preventing the world from getting there]
1. [obstacle]
2. [obstacle]

**Act 5 — The path (us):**
[How we are the means by which buyers get to the promised land]
[Frame as method/movement, not just product]

---

*One-paragraph version (for pitches, intros, emails):*
[Compressed narrative — should still feel like a story, not a tagline]

*Derivatives to produce:*
- [ ] Pitch deck spine — 12 slides
- [ ] Sales deck — 8 slides
- [ ] Website hero — H1 + subhead + CTA
- [ ] Founder LinkedIn post manifesto — 600 words
- [ ] Recruiting page — narrative-led intro
- [ ] Investor update template — narrative-led opening
```

## Connectors

- Slack (delivery)
- CMS (for website implementation)
- Sales / marketing materials

## Gotchas

- Narrative ≠ tagline. A tagline is what you say; narrative is what you mean.
- Test with skeptics — narrative that wins with believers is easy; one that wins with skeptics is real
- Distribute and enforce internally — narrative dies when each function rewrites it
- Refresh every 2-3 years — markets shift, narratives age
